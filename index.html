Remembering Amrita

Amrita Sher-Gil: Portrait of an Artist as a Young Woman

Sher-Gil knew the power of a stylised public image but her life was dedicated to her art. Remembering a pioneer of modern Indian art on her 110th birthday. In the letters that painter Amrita Sher-Gil wrote to friends and family, two subjects crisscross like lace-work. Her belief that her art was Indian because its spirit was Indian even though she was of mixed parentage—and her excitement over the new fashions with which she would project her public persona. Her parents, and at times, her sister Indira, were willing partners in the creation of this personal style—two of Sher-Gil’s most known photographs, clicked by her father Umrao Singh, show her in a sari with a broad embellished border and paired with a brocade blouse, and another standing in the sun in a sari speckled with gold butis. Friends from the art fraternity were also drafted into this image-making. For instance, Karl Khandalavala, a well-known Bombay (now Mumbai) art critic, who was one of her strongest and early champions. In a letter to him outlining her progress on a large-format painting, the conversation segues into a playful “So you like my new cape?”, and back to a self-portrait she had made which, she suggested, could be used with a piece in the Illustrated Weekly on her talents as a portrait painter. Amrita Sher-Gil painting sold for Rs 37.8 cr

A painting by Amrita Sher-Gil, 'In the Ladies' Enclosure' (1938), was sold for Rs 37.8 crore (USD 5.14 million) at the Saffronart auction house sale in Mumbai on Tuesday. This is the second most expensive artwork by an Indian.
